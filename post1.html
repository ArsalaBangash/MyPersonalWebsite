Going at it Alone

A native development approach to the app and the inspiration behind my ambitious project

"You don't understand bro, I've wanted this to exist for so long. I've wanted this to exist before I had a smartphone. I wanted something to help me out when I was prepping for the SATs; 20 or more questions in 25 minutes without a calculator...people need help with getting better at mental math." - Arsala, some time in November, probably in the Stats Room

For years now, I've wanted to create a mobile application that would help students and the general public improve their ability to perform rapid mental math. This application wouldn't be limited to the mundanity of Math's 4 basic operators, and would have users solving coordinate geometry problems and factorising quadratic polynomials using only their noggin. However, after a painstaking research process, I arrived at the conclusion that an app cannot be wished into existence. That is why during the summer of 2016, I took my first concrete steps to making this project a tangible reality.

I was learning Android development with a friend so that we could publish our first game together, and as I learned key Android development concepts, I applied them to little side projects of mine. The first tiny build of ConfirMAT (the title is still in progress) was a by-product of me learning how grid layouts work, and it involved simple addition and subtraction. However even as I coded the simple addition and subtraction components of the app, I saw the potential it had.

It seemed as if I had an epiphany every week about which new cross-platform mobile technology I would use for development. My framework-swings would take me from web-app to React Native to (Insert arbitrary Javascript based Mobile development tool here). Finally, one night, after a tonne of Computational Theory, I sat down and coded Division and Multiplication into the app using our good ol' Android Studio. Seeing actual progress as I played on my phone trumped all the theoretical advances I had made when deciding the one framework that was going to save me time and money when eventually having to develop for the iHipsters. I thus decided to go native, and tailor the mental math improvement to each platform.

It was naive of me to think that most people would share my passion for this project. It took countless people turning me down (okay it was more like five people) to make me realise that this project wouldn't get done by workers, but rather by enthusiastic developers. But alas, there exists only one for now, and he must go at it alone.
